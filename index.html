About Kentucky

Kentucky is a state located in the east-central region of the United States. It became the 15th state admitted to the Union on June 1, 1792. Bordered by seven states — Illinois, Indiana, Ohio, West Virginia, Virginia, Tennessee, and Missouri — Kentucky is known for its stunning landscapes, thoroughbred horse farms, bourbon distilleries, and coal mining heritage.

The state capital is Frankfort, while its largest city is Louisville, home to the world-famous Kentucky Derby. The state nickname, the Bluegrass State, comes from the bluegrass that grows throughout the region, giving the pastures a distinctive blue-green hue.

History Highlights

Early Exploration (1750s–1770s)

European-American explorers began entering the region in the 1750s. Daniel Boone's expeditions in the late 1760s and his role in blazing the Wilderness Road through the Cumberland Gap opened Kentucky to large-scale settlement from the eastern colonies.

Statehood & Growth (1792–1860)

Kentucky became the first U.S. state west of the Appalachian Mountains in 1792. The fertile land attracted thousands of settlers, and agriculture — especially hemp, tobacco, and horse breeding — drove rapid economic growth throughout the 19th century.

Civil War Era (1861–1865)

Kentucky declared neutrality at the start of the Civil War, but was claimed by both the Union and the Confederacy. Several major battles were fought on Kentucky soil, including the Battle of Perryville in 1862, and the state ultimately remained in the Union.

Modern Kentucky (20th Century–Present)

The 20th century brought industrialization, coal mining booms, and expansion of the bourbon and horse industries. Today Kentucky is known for its vibrant tourism, University of Kentucky and University of Louisville athletics, and its growing technology and manufacturing sectors.
